    Car dealers reject government's plan to reduce imported vehicles' age limit to 5 years

    - The government had proposed to reduce the age limit of imported used cars from eight to five years then three years - The new regulation is expected to take effect from July 2019 in a gradual plan to rid the country of used vehicles

    22 Mar 2024, 00:00

    - The government had proposed to reduce the age limit of imported used cars from eight to five years then three years

    - The new regulation is expected to take effect from July 2019 in a gradual plan to rid the country of used vehicles

    - Trade CS Peter Munya said the regulation would affect vehicles above 1500CC/1.5 litre engine capacity

    - Second-hand car dealers against the proposal argued it would hurt the local motor industry

    Importers of used cars have rejected a proposal by the government to slash age limit of imported second-hand cars from the current eight years to five then three years by 2021 arguing such a move would cripple the local motor industry.

    The importers asked the government to drop the contentious Draft National Automotive Policy which seeks to phase out imported used vehicles in the local motor industry.

    Speaking through their umbrella body, the Kenya Auto Bazaar Association (KABA) on Sunday, February 10, the second-hand car dealers argued reducing the age limit of the imported vehicles would soar up prices of vehicles in the country.

    "Reducing the age of all used imported cars from eight years to five years by 2019, then to three years by 2021 is a bad policy for the country. If implemented, this will mean that a car that currently costs KSh 500,000 will now cost at least KSh 1.2 million,” argued KABA's chairman, John Kipchumba.

    READ ALSO: Fear grips importers as government plans to reduce second car import age to 5 years

    Kipchumba said they would only stop importing used cars if the government sets up industries where 100% of the vehicles can be assembled locally, with 40% of the vehicle components also manufactured locally.

    “They have to demonstrate to us that they have the capacity to manufacture vehicles here in Kenya so that we can stop importing second-hand cars,” he was quoted by Citizen TV.

    READ ALSO: How much does it cost to import a car from Japan to Kenya?

    The government's new regulation seeking to reduce the age limit of imported second-hand vehicles is expected to take effect from July 2019 in a gradual plan to rid the country of used vehicles.

    Trade and Industry Cabinet Secretary (CS) Peter Munya said the proposed policy would affect vehicles above 1500CC/1.5 litre engine capacity.

    This means Kenyans intending to import such vehicles will be forced to part with more cash to match the value of brand new cars.

    "People who would want to purchase cars with bigger engine capacities will have to pay more because they will only be allowed to bring in the newer five-year-old vehicles," Munya said.

    READ ALSO: Personal savings account or chama? Pros and cons

    The government's plan, TUKO.co.ke understands, is to gradually phase out used vehicles and allow only brand new ones in the local motor market.

    This new law, Munya said, is part of the government's strategy to ignite and sustain local manufacturing of vehicles.

    "Those who will need the fuel guzzlers need to have the financial muscle to get newer cars. Otherwise they will have to settle for smaller cars as we explore options of having locally assembled vehicles," the CS said.
